Personal Pronouns

The second type of pronoun we are going to look at is the personal pronoun, which are pronouns that relate to person, i.e. first, second, and third. In English they may be divided into subject, object, possessive and reflexive/intensive forms. English also has a possessive adjective. These pronouns and the related adjective represent the remnants of a gendered language such as Latin. Our nouns may no longer have gender attached to them, but our pronouns still do (e.g. he/she/it). They also show remnants of case (e.g. subject, object). To ensure you understand how they are used in Latin, let’s look at the English pronouns and how they are used first. This is how they are used:

Subject Pronoun: “I am teaching you.” “I” is doing the action.

Object Pronoun: “You are thanking me.” “Me” is receiving the action

Possessive Pronoun: “Here is a book. It is mine.” “Mine” is a pronoun replacing “my book”.

Possessive Adjective: “That is my book.” “My” is an adjective modifying the noun “book”.

Reflexive Pronoun: “I bought the book for myself.” “Myself” in this context is reflecting the subject “I” to show that the same person doing the action, is also receiving the action.

Intensive Pronoun: “I bought the book myself.” “Myself” in this context is emphasising that the subject “I” bought the book, ‘in person’, not someone else.

Latin is also able to say all these things, but the forms used are structured somewhat differently. The English subject pronoun is the Latin personal pronoun in the nominative case; the English object pronoun is the Latin personal pronoun in the accusative case; the English possessive pronoun is not a separate entity in Latin, but the substantive of the possessive adjective; and the Latin reflexive pronoun and intensive pronoun are two entirely separate words. Personal Pronouns

Since pronouns stand in place of a noun, they are used in the same way as a noun is used, i.e. as subjects, direct objects, indirect objects, objects of a preposition, etc. and therefore exist in all five cases, e.g. the first-person singular pronouns in Latin are ego (I: subject), me (me: object), mei (of me: *explanation pending!*), mihi (to/for me: indirect object), and me (by/with/from me: object of a preposition). These definitions can then be extrapolated to the second-person singular: tu (you), te (you), tui, tibi, te; the first-person plural: nos (we), nos (us), nostrum/nostri, nobis, nobis; and the second-person plural: vos (you), vos (you), vestrum/vestri, vobis, vobis.

Don’t forget though, that every Latin finite verb has the person and number embedded in it as an ending. Therefore, the nominative pronoun is not generally required in a sentence, e.g. intellego stands alone without a pronoun and means “I understand” and intellegimus, “we understand”, etc. When then, does Latin use its nominative personal pronoun? To emphasise the pronoun embedded in the verb. So, if you’ve just had an ‘aha! moment’ reading this, then you might want to say “Ego intellego!” (I!!! understand) rather than just plain garden-variety “Intellego.” (I understand).

Okay, now that tu intellegis all the above, I’ll explain the genitive case of the personal pronouns (mei, tui, nostrum/nostri, vestrum/vestri), as promised above. They do not show possession, e.g. although mei does translate as “of me”, it does not mean “my”. If I want to say “my book” I would use the possessive adjective meus, a, um (liber meus) which is explained in the next section.

What the genitive case of the personal pronoun is used for, is as a partitive genitive and an objective genitive. Both are explained on the page “Uses of the Genitive Case”, but I will show how they relate specifically to personal pronouns here. Used as a partitive genitive, mei for example, can imply “some part of me”, e.g. part of me (pars mei) understands this”. Used as an objective genitive, mei is the object of a noun which has fundamentally a verbal sense, e.g. “the love of me”: amor mei. In the plural, for a reason I do not know, Latin has a different form of the genitive for each of these uses. Nostrum and vestrum are used as partitive genitives, e.g. “three thousand of us”: tria milia nostrum; while nostri and vestri are used as objective genitives, “the love of us”: amor nostri.

And finally, the ablative case. An idiom to be aware of about the personal pronoun is when it is used as the object of the preposition cum. Normally, cum is followed by an ablative noun, e.g. “with a friend”: cum amico. However, if I want to say “with me/you/us/you”, cum is preceded by the ablative pronoun and the words are joined together to become one word: mecum, tecum, nobiscum, vobiscum.

So, what is missing here? The third-person personal pronoun! In Latin there isn’t one, in either the singular or plural. No “he”, “she”, “it”, or “they” as such. The closest equivalent to these English words is the weak demonstrative pronoun is, ea, id. This pronoun is fully declined on the Demonstrative Pronouns page. When used to modify a noun, it means “this/that noun”, e.g. “this book”: is liber; “this ship”: ea navis; “this town”: id oppidum. When used alone like a substantive (see Latin Adjectives 1), where is means “this male person”, ea means “this female person” and id means “this thing”, we can translate that into our English third-person pronouns, “he”, “she”, and “it” respectively, e.g. Femina scribit. Ea est magistra mea: “A woman is writing. She (this woman) is my teacher.”

Keep in mind that ‘is, ea, id’ is a demonstrative pronoun/adjective, not a personal pronoun, so the issues mentioned above for the first- and second-person pronouns do not apply for the third-person. That is, the nominative case is not emphatic; the genitive case does show possession; and the ablative when used as an object of the preposition cum, does not form the mecum, tecum business, but just plain, ordinary cum eo, cum eā, cum iis.

Possessive Adjectives

The possessive adjectives are all first/second declension adjectives which decline accordingly. Just note that the nominative masculine forms noster and vester both contract to form the respective bases nostr- and vestr-, otherwise all is regular. Meus means “my”; tuus, “your” (singular); suus, both the singular “her/his/its” and the plural “their”; noster, “our”; and vester, “your” (plural).

They all function like a regular adjective modifying a noun and therefore need to agree with the noun in gender, number and case., e.g. “my (female) friend”: amica mea; “my (female) friends”: amicae meae; “my (male) friend”: amicus meus; “my (male) friends”: amici mei; “my arm”: bracchium meum; “my arms”: bracchia mea; “I love my (female) friend”: amicam meam amo; etc. That is, the possessive adjective agrees with the gender, number and case of the noun it is modifying, not with those of the person/s doing the possessing.

The third person possessive adjective suus, needs further explanation as to its usage, and therefore, translation. This adjective is a reflexive adjective and is only ever used to refer back to the third-person subject of the sentence, e.g. “the teacher praises her (own) student”, i.e. a student in her own class, not in another teacher’s class: magistra discipulam suam laudat. This explains why the same word suus can be used in both the singular (his/her/its) and plural (their). If the subject is plural, then suus must mean “their”, the English third-person plural possessive adjective, e.g. “the teachers praise their (own) student”: magistrae discipulam suam laudant. If a teacher is praising a student of another teacher, i.e. not the subject of the sentence, the reflexive adjective cannot be used and the genitive form eius of the demonstrative pronoun ‘is, ea, id’ is used instead, e.g. magistra discipulam eius laudat. As explained above regarding the use of is, ea, id as a substantive, what this last sentence is really saying is “the teacher praises her (that other teacher’s) student”. It may seem a confusing concept at first, because it is totally different to the English idiom. However, it actually leads to less confusion than the often ambiguous English practice of using the same possessive adjective to mean both things. So, it is really a score to the Romans, I think.

Reflexive Pronouns

So, while we are on a roll with reflexive possessive adjectives, let’s talk about reflexive pronouns. Just as explained above, reflexive pronouns reflect the subject of the sentence. In English, for the first and second persons, we append the suffix “-self” (singular) and “-selves” (plural) to the possessive adjective, i.e. “myself, yourself, ourselves, yourselves”. For the third person we append the same suffixes to the object pronoun, i.e. “him/her/itself, themselves”.

In Latin, for the first and second persons, the reflexive pronoun is the same as the personal pronoun. This makes a lot of sense and why this isn’t the situation in English I don’t know, e.g. “I see myself” could easily be just “I (subject pronoun) see me (object pronoun)” and mean the same thing. And the same for “you see yourself”. Why not just “you see you”? So, another score to the Romans! But while it is obvious who “I” and “you” are in a sentence, it is not the same for the third person where “him/her” could refer either to the subject or to another person, e.g. “he sees him” does require a special pronoun, e.g. “himself” to show reflection of the subject. Consequently, Latin does use a special third person reflexive pronoun.

Therefore, for the first and second persons there are no new pronouns to learn! Just the same ones as the personal pronouns, but there are only four cases: accusative, genitive, dative, and ablative. The reason there is no nominative case is that since they refer to the subject, they can’t be the subject. The only new pronouns you need to learn are for the third person, and again, only the four cases: accusative (se), genitive (sui), dative (sibi), and ablative (se). And for the same reason as explained under possessive adjectives, these are used for both the singular and plural, i.e. se, sui, sibi, se will translate as “himself”, “herself”, “itself”, or “themselves” depending on the gender and number of the subject.

Also note that since se, sui, sibi, se is a personal pronoun, the ablative behaves in the same way as me, te, nobis, and vobis, when it is the object of the preposition cum, i.e. it forms the single word secum to mean “with himself”, “with herself”, “with itself”, “with themselves”.

Intensive Pronoun

An intensive pronoun emphasises that someone performed an action ‘in person’. “I myself, have written this document” tells you quite emphatically that it was me who wrote it; I didn’t pay or coerce anybody else to write it for me. In English, the intensive pronoun is the same as the reflexive pronoun, e.g. “myself” can be either reflexive or intensive and only the context of the sentence will allow you to know which one it is, and it isn’t always easy to tell. Latin once again, makes it much plainer. Two entirely different words! The Romans score yet another point. The Latin intensive pronoun/adjective is ipse, ipsa, ipsum. It is shown fully declined in Table 6 above. It shouldn’t be too difficult to memorise as the endings are identical to the demonstrative pronoun/adjective ille, illa, illud, except for the nominative and accusative neuter which ends with the more regular second declension ending -um rather than the irregular -ud. Note that it has the same -ius genitive and -i dative endings of all the demonstrative pronouns.
